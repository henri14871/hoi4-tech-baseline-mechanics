[h1]Arms Race Mechanics[/h1]

[h2]The Problem[/h2]
It's 1943. The problem is not that the AI never researches. It does. The problem is that too many countries, including major powers, lag several years behind where they should be in key branches. A large country may be mostly fine in infantry and industry, but still be strangely behind in air, armor, radar, or naval development despite having the slots and economy to keep up.

HOI4's AI research often leaves countries noticeably behind what their economy, military scale, and war involvement would suggest. The result is a world that becomes less competitive and less believable as the game goes on, not because research is absent, but because it is spread and prioritized badly enough to create major branch gaps.

[h2]The Fix[/h2]
Arms Race Mechanics gives every nation a credible technological baseline based on its power, industry, and military profile. Major industrial powers stay closer to the frontier across the branches they can realistically support. Regional states remain relevant in their strongest areas. Minor countries still lag behind, but they no longer fall so far off the curve that they stop mattering.

[b]Your research game stays relevant.[/b] ARM runs AI-only by default, but it also works well if you enable it for players. You still decide what to rush, what to delay, and how to specialize. ARM raises the baseline and keeps countries closer to the curve, so research choices matter more because the competition is stronger.

[h2]What Changes In Your Game[/h2]
[list]
[*] [b]AI nations stay closer to the curve.[/b] The issue is no longer big countries mysteriously running years behind in key branches they should be able to maintain.
[*] [b]Wars get harder and last longer.[/b] Nations under pressure catch up faster. Losing a war doesn't mean a tech death spiral. It means a country has a chance to stay relevant and keep fighting.
[*] [b]Minor nations matter.[/b] Romania gets artillery. Siam gets basic fighters. They're not world leaders, but they're not pushovers either. Lend-lease and puppets actually make strategic sense.
[*] [b]Late game stays competitive.[/b] The anti-death-spiral system and catch-up bonuses mean 1944-45 wars are decided more by strategy and execution, not by one side having jets while the other still looks stuck in the interwar period.
[*] [b]Your research choices matter more, not less.[/b] When the baseline is higher, the edge you get from smart research timing and specialization becomes the real differentiator.
[/list]

[h2]How It Works[/h2]
Every nation is scored on five dimensions: economy, science, mobilization, resources, and war posture. That score places them into one of six power tiers, from Micro to Superpower. Each tier sets a technology lag (how many years behind the frontier) and a quarterly grant cap (how many techs per cycle).

On top of global power, four [b]branch competence[/b] scores (Land, Air, Naval, Industry) control which specific tech categories a nation receives. A country with 50 dockyards and no air force gets destroyers and cruisers, not fighters. A landlocked nation with heavy industry gets tanks and artillery, not submarines. Tech grants follow what a country can plausibly support, not a flat one-size-fits-all tech dump.

Nations at war, in factions with stronger allies, or losing badly get catch-up bonuses that reduce their tech lag. Puppet nations benefit from their overlord's strength. The system prevents the snowball effect where one lost battle leads to permanent technological irrelevance.
